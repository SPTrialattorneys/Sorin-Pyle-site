Your Rights in Different Situations

üè† Home Searches - 4th Amendment

‚ñº

Your home is your castle under the law. Police generally need a warrant to enter.

  • Police MUST have a warrant signed by a judge (with few exceptions)
  • You can refuse entry without a warrant
  • Ask to see and read the warrant - it must specify what they can search
  • Do NOT consent to a search - make them use the warrant
  • Step outside to talk (don't let them see inside)
Magic Words:

"I do not consent to any searches. I want to see a warrant."

Exceptions when they don't need a warrant: Hot pursuit, immediate danger, destroying evidence, or if you consent (DON'T!).

ü§ê Right to Remain Silent - 5th Amendment

‚ñº

You cannot be forced to be a witness against yourself. This is absolute.

  • You NEVER have to answer police questions
  • Silence cannot be used against you IF you invoke it
  • You must clearly state you're using this right
  • This applies before, during, and after arrest
  • Also applies to written statements
Magic Words:

"I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent."

"I will not answer any questions without my attorney present."

Critical: Just being quiet isn't enough - you MUST verbally invoke your right!

‚öñÔ∏è Right to an Attorney - 6th Amendment

‚ñº

You have the right to legal representation at all critical stages.

  • You can request an attorney at ANY time
  • Once requested, police must stop questioning
  • If you can't afford one, one will be appointed
  • You can have an attorney present during questioning
  • This right applies even before charges are filed
Magic Words:

"I want to speak to my attorney."

"I will not answer questions without my lawyer present."

Remember: Having a lawyer is not an admission of guilt - it's protecting your rights!

üöó Vehicle Searches

‚ñº

Vehicles have less protection than homes, but you still have rights:

  • Police need probable cause OR your consent
  • You can refuse consent to search
  • Lock your car and close windows if asked to exit
  • They can look through windows (plain view)
  • K9 sniff is allowed during a traffic stop (but can't extend stop unreasonably)

Say clearly: "Officer, I do not consent to any searches of my vehicle."

Note: They may search anyway if they claim probable cause, but your refusal can help in court.

üì± Phone & Digital Privacy

‚ñº

Your phone has strong constitutional protection:

  • Police need a warrant to search your phone (even if arrested)
  • You do NOT have to provide passwords or unlock codes
  • Biometrics (fingerprint/face) may be compelled - use passcode instead
  • They cannot force you to unlock your phone without a court order
  • Cloud data also requires a warrant

Best practice: Keep phone locked, enable auto-lock, don't consent to any searches.

Say: "I do not consent to a search of my phone or digital devices."

üö∂ Street Encounters

‚ñº

Know the three types of police encounters:

  • Consensual: You're free to leave anytime
  • Detention: Brief stop with reasonable suspicion
  • Arrest: Probable cause required
Always Ask:

"Am I free to leave?"

If yes ‚Üí "I'm leaving now."

If no ‚Üí "I'm invoking my right to remain silent. I want a lawyer."

Important: Never run, but you can walk away if not detained.

üé• Recording Police

‚ñº

You have a First Amendment right to record police in public:

  • You can openly record any police interaction in public
  • This includes your own or others' interactions
  • Keep a safe distance - don't interfere
  • You don't need to tell them you're recording (but it may help)
  • They cannot delete your recordings or take your phone without a warrant

Tips: Use cloud backup, livestream if possible, stay calm and obvious about recording.

If challenged: "I have a First Amendment right to record in public."
