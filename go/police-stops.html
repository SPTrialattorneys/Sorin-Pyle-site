Your Rights During Police Stops

Keep this information saved. You have constitutional rights during any police encounter in Michigan. Understanding these rights can protect you from self-incrimination and ensure fair treatment under the law.

Michigan law requires specific conditions for a valid traffic stop:

  • You must provide license, registration, and proof of insurance
  • You are NOT required to answer questions about where you're going or coming from
  • You can refuse consent to search your vehicle
  • Record the interaction if you feel unsafe

Say clearly: "Officer, I prefer to exercise my right to remain silent."

üîç If Police Want to Search

‚ñº

The Fourth Amendment protects you from unreasonable searches:

  • You can refuse consent to any search
  • Police need probable cause or a warrant for most searches
  • Never physically resist - state your refusal clearly
  • If they search anyway, your lawyer can challenge it later

Say clearly: "Officer, I do not consent to any searches."

üöî If You're Arrested

‚ñº

Remember these critical points if arrested:

  • Remain silent except to request an attorney
  • Do not resist, even if you believe the arrest is wrong
  • Do not sign anything without your lawyer present
  • Remember badge numbers and patrol car numbers
  • Request a phone call as soon as possible

Say only: "I am exercising my right to remain silent. I want to speak to my lawyer."

üì± Your Right to Record

‚ñº

In Michigan, you have the right to record police interactions:

  • You can openly record police in public spaces
  • Inform the officer you are recording for everyone's safety
  • Do not interfere with their duties while recording
  • Save recordings to the cloud immediately if possible

Note: Recording protects both you and the officer by creating an accurate record.
