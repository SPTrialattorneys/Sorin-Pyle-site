Limited Public Transportation = Higher DUI Risk

Unlike larger university towns, Big Rapids has very limited late-night public transportation options. There's no Uber saturation, limited taxi service, and minimal late-night bus routes. This creates a critical problem:

  • Students drive to parties, bars, or friends' houses
  • After drinking, they have no safe way home except driving
  • Result: Higher DUI arrest rates for Ferris students than at urban universities

For professional program students, a DUI conviction can be career-ending:

  • Pharmacy students: DEA denies licenses for DUI (involves drugs/alcohol)
  • Optometry students: State licensing board scrutiny
  • Engineering students: Security clearances denied
  • License suspension: How will you get to campus, clinical rotations, or internships?

Understanding Dual Proceedings: Criminal Court vs. Ferris State Discipline

Here's what most Ferris students don't realize: getting arrested doesn't just mean criminal court. You may also face a completely separate disciplinary process through Ferris State's Student Conduct Office.

This means you could be fighting on two fronts simultaneously:

Criminal Court Proceedings (77th District Court / 49th Circuit Court)

  • Prosecuted by Mecosta County Prosecutor's Office
  • Constitutional rights apply (right to remain silent, right to attorney, etc.)
  • Burden of proof: "Beyond a reasonable doubt"
  • Potential outcomes: Conviction, plea deal, dismissal, diversion programs
  • Consequences: Jail time, fines, probation, permanent criminal record

Ferris State Student Conduct Proceedings

  • Conducted by Ferris State Student Conduct Office
  • Lower burden of proof: "Preponderance of the evidence" (more likely than not)
  • Limited due process rights compared to criminal court
  • Potential outcomes: Warning, probation, suspension, expulsion
  • Consequences: Loss of housing, removal from professional programs, notation on transcript, expulsion

Critical: Ferris can discipline you even if you're found not guilty in criminal court. The university uses a lower standard of evidence. They can also take action for off-campus conduct.

Professional program reporting requirements: Many Ferris programs require students to self-report arrests:

  • Pharmacy students: Must report arrests to program directors (accreditation requirement)
  • Optometry students: Required to disclose for clinical rotations and board applications
  • Engineering students: Some programs require disclosure for co-op placements
  • Other professional programs: May have reporting requirements

Failing to report when required can result in immediate dismissal from your program - even if the criminal charges are eventually dropped. This creates a dilemma: You must report, but reporting without legal counsel can damage both your criminal case and your academic standing.

Solution: Consult with a criminal defense attorney BEFORE reporting to your program. We can help you fulfill reporting obligations while protecting your rights.

Common Criminal Charges for Ferris State Students

Minor in Possession of Alcohol (MIP)

MIP is the most common charge for Ferris students. Big Rapids police regularly patrol off-campus housing areas and party locations.

Michigan MIP Law (effective 2018):

  • First offense: Civil infraction (not a criminal misdemeanor), fine up to $100
  • Second offense: Misdemeanor, up to 30 days in jail, doubled fines
  • Third offense: Misdemeanor, up to 60 days in jail, up to $500 fine

Why MIP is serious for Ferris professional students:

  • Pharmacy programs view alcohol violations as substance abuse indicators
  • State licensing boards ask about alcohol-related offenses
  • Multiple MIPs indicate pattern of substance use - can affect licensure
  • DEA background checks include all alcohol-related offenses
  • Ferris conduct office may impose additional discipline

Drug Possession Charges - CAREER-ENDING for Pharmacy Students

Even though Michigan legalized recreational marijuana, it's still illegal for anyone under 21. But more importantly, ANY drug conviction is absolutely career-ending for pharmacy students.

Why drug charges destroy pharmacy careers:

  • DEA background checks: The Drug Enforcement Administration must approve all pharmacy licenses. Drug convictions = automatic denial
  • Michigan Board of Pharmacy: State licensing board denies licenses for drug convictions
  • Controlled substances handling: Pharmacists handle Schedule II-V drugs daily. Anyone with drug convictions cannot be trusted with controlled substances
  • No appeals process: DEA denials are permanent. There is no workaround, no exceptions, no appeal
  • Years of education wasted: Ferris pharmacy program is 6-8 years (pre-pharmacy + PharmD). A drug conviction means all that time and money is lost

Marijuana charges for students under 21:

  • Use or possession by person under 21: Civil infraction, $100 fine (first offense)
  • Second offense: Civil infraction, $500 fine
  • But: Still appears on background checks and disqualifies from pharmacy licensure

Other drug charges (prescription pills, cocaine, MDMA, etc.):

  • Immediate removal from pharmacy program
  • Permanent DEA and state board denial
  • Loss of federal financial aid eligibility
  • Criminal record with potential jail time

For optometry, engineering, and other students: Drug convictions still affect professional licensing, security clearances, and employment background checks. While not always automatic disqualifiers like in pharmacy, they create significant barriers.

Fake ID Possession and Use - Crime of Dishonesty

Using a fake ID to buy alcohol or get into Big Rapids bars is a misdemeanor in Michigan. Beyond the legal consequences, fake IDs represent crimes of dishonesty - particularly problematic for students pursuing licensed professions.

Why fake IDs are career-ending for professional students:

  • Pharmacy licensure: Michigan Board of Pharmacy views dishonesty as disqualifying character flaw
  • Optometry licensure: State board scrutinizes integrity and trustworthiness
  • Engineering PE license: Professional Engineer ethics boards review dishonesty offenses
  • CPA certification: Accounting students cannot become CPAs with dishonesty convictions

Professional licensing boards across all fields view crimes of dishonesty (fake IDs, fraud, theft) as indicators of character unfitness. These convictions raise questions about your trustworthiness in positions requiring public trust.

DUI/OWI Charges - Particularly Common in Rural Big Rapids

As discussed earlier, limited public transportation in Big Rapids results in higher DUI rates. Ferris students ages 21-22 face DUI/OWI charges when driving after drinking.

Michigan's Zero Tolerance Law for drivers under 21: If you're under 21, you can be charged with OWI if your BAC is 0.02% or higher (essentially any alcohol).

Why DUI is particularly devastating for Ferris students:

  • Pharmacy students: DEA views DUI as substance abuse issue - can deny licensure
  • Optometry students: State licensing board scrutiny
  • Engineering students: Security clearances for defense contractors denied
  • All students: License suspension makes it impossible to attend classes, clinical rotations, or internships in rural Big Rapids
  • No Uber option: Unlike urban schools, you can't rely on rideshare to compensate for license loss

Domestic Violence Charges

College dating relationships can become volatile. Domestic violence charges at Ferris often arise from arguments in off-campus housing or residence halls.

Ferris students face unique domestic violence consequences:

  • Removal from campus housing
  • No-contact orders preventing class attendance
  • Title IX investigation parallel to criminal case
  • Suspension even before criminal case resolves
  • Professional licensing impact: Violence convictions can disqualify from pharmacy, optometry, engineering licenses

Other Common Charges

  • Disorderly conduct: Fighting, loud behavior at parties
  • House party violations: Big Rapids police frequently break up large parties
  • Open intoxicant: Walking with open alcohol containers
  • Retail fraud: Shoplifting (dishonesty offense affecting licensing)

Professional Licensing Consequences by Program

College of Pharmacy (PharmD Program)

This is the most critical section for pharmacy students. Read carefully.

The Ferris College of Pharmacy leads to a Doctor of Pharmacy (PharmD) degree. To practice as a pharmacist, you must be licensed by:

  • Michigan Board of Pharmacy: State licensing authority
  • Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA): Federal controlled substances license

Criminal convictions that WILL prevent pharmacy licensure:

  • Any drug conviction: Marijuana, prescription pills without a prescription, cocaine, MDMA - all disqualifying
  • Theft convictions: Cannot be trusted handling medications and inventory
  • Fraud/dishonesty: Fake IDs, prescription fraud, insurance fraud
  • DUI (sometimes): DEA may view as substance abuse issue
  • Multiple alcohol violations: Pattern indicates substance abuse

DEA Background Check Process:

  1. You complete 6-8 years of education at Ferris (pre-pharmacy + PharmD)
  2. You pass the NAPLEX and MPJE exams
  3. You apply for Michigan pharmacy license (Board reviews criminal history)
  4. If approved by state, you apply for DEA registration (required to dispense controlled substances)
  5. DEA denies registration if you have drug convictions - END OF CAREER

Timeline matters: Even if the Michigan Board eventually approves you after appeals (which can take years), the DEA will still deny your federal registration. You cannot practice pharmacy in any meaningful capacity without DEA registration.

There are no workarounds. No exceptions. No appeals. A drug conviction ends your pharmacy career permanently.

Michigan College of Optometry

Ferris is home to Michigan's ONLY College of Optometry. If you're kicked out of this program or denied licensure, you must:

  • Apply to out-of-state optometry schools (extremely competitive, expensive)
  • Or abandon your optometry career entirely

Michigan Board of Optometry licensing considerations:

  • Reviews all criminal history
  • Drug convictions can result in license denial
  • Crimes of dishonesty (theft, fraud, fake IDs) heavily scrutinized
  • Violence convictions raise patient safety concerns
  • Even approved applications can be delayed for years during investigation

Clinical rotations: Optometry students must complete clinical rotations at hospitals, clinics, and optometry practices. Background checks are required. Criminal charges can prevent placement, making degree completion impossible.

College of Engineering Technology

Engineering students often pursue Professional Engineer (PE) licensure and careers requiring security clearances:

  • PE licensure: State licensing board reviews criminal history; ethics requirements
  • Security clearances: Defense contractors, government positions require clearances - drug convictions, DUI, theft all disqualifying
  • Corporate background checks: Major engineering firms conduct thorough screening
  • International work: Overseas engineering projects require work visas - criminal records prevent visa approval

College of Business

  • CPA certification: Accounting students must disclose criminal history; dishonesty offenses prevent certification
  • Finance positions: Banks, investment firms conduct FBI background checks
  • Management careers: Leadership positions require trustworthiness - criminal records raise concerns

Kendall College of Art and Design

While Kendall students don't face professional licensing, criminal records still affect careers:

  • International opportunities: Study abroad, artist residencies, international exhibitions require visas - criminal records prevent approval
  • Corporate design positions: Major firms conduct background checks
  • Agency work: Advertising, design, marketing agencies screen for theft and drug convictions
  • Freelance limitations: Some clients require background checks for contractors

PGA Golf Management Program

Ferris has the nation's first PGA Golf Management program. Criminal records can prevent PGA membership and career placement in golf management.

Protecting Your Financial Aid and Scholarships

Federal Financial Aid

  • Alcohol offenses (MIP, OWI): Generally do NOT affect federal aid eligibility
  • Drug convictions: WILL affect FAFSA eligibility immediately
  • Must report drug convictions on FAFSA applications
  • Period of ineligibility depends on offense type and number of convictions
  • Can regain eligibility through approved drug rehabilitation programs

Ferris State Institutional Scholarships

  • Merit scholarships require "good standing" (not on disciplinary probation)
  • Departmental scholarships (pharmacy, optometry, engineering) have conduct requirements
  • Professional program scholarships often explicitly prohibit criminal conduct
  • Once lost, scholarships are rarely reinstated even if charges are dismissed

Graduate School and Professional Doctorate Applications

Many Ferris programs are professional doctorates (PharmD, OD). Criminal records affect both degree completion and licensure:

  • Professional doctorate programs: Criminal history questions on applications
  • Residency programs: Post-graduate pharmacy residencies require background checks
  • Fellowship programs: Competitive programs screen out applicants with records
  • Other graduate schools: If you leave Ferris, criminal records affect transfer applications

International Student Immigration Consequences

Ferris enrolls international students. International students on F-1 visas face severe consequences from criminal charges:

Visa Status Risks

  • Criminal convictions can result in visa revocation
  • Drug convictions can trigger deportation proceedings
  • Crimes of moral turpitude can make you inadmissible

Maintaining Legal Status

  • If Ferris suspends you, you lose F-1 status (no longer enrolled full-time)
  • Loss of F-1 status requires immediate departure from the U.S.

International students must consult with both a criminal defense attorney and an immigration attorney.

Frequently Asked Questions - Ferris State Student Defense

Can I become a pharmacist with a drug conviction?

No. The Michigan Board of Pharmacy and the DEA conduct extensive background checks. Any drug conviction will likely result in permanent denial of pharmacy licensure. The DEA requires pharmacists to have clean records to handle controlled substances. Even if the Michigan Board eventually approves your application after years of appeals, the DEA background check will disqualify you. For Ferris pharmacy students, a drug conviction ends your career before it starts.

Will a criminal charge prevent me from becoming an optometrist?

It can. The Michigan Board of Optometry reviews all criminal history. Drug convictions, crimes of dishonesty (theft, fraud, fake IDs), and violence convictions can result in license denial. Ferris is Michigan's only optometry program - if you're removed or denied licensure, you must apply to out-of-state programs or abandon your optometry career.

Are there many attorneys in Big Rapids who handle student cases?

Big Rapids is a rural community with limited legal resources. Many local attorneys practice general law rather than specializing in criminal defense. This is actually an opportunity - you can work with experienced criminal defense attorneys from Holland who regularly practice in Big Rapids courts. We frequently appear in 77th District Court and know the judges and prosecutors.

Should I tell my pharmacy program director about my arrest?

Many professional programs have mandatory reporting requirements. Failing to report when required can result in immediate dismissal. However, reporting without legal counsel can also create problems. You should consult with a criminal defense attorney BEFORE speaking with program administrators. We can help you understand your reporting obligations and advise you on how to fulfill them while protecting your rights.
